4.1 The normal distribution

Let us assume that each patient not yet admitted has a fixed but unknown time and that the times are normally distributed in the population (of unseen patients) with some unknown mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\).

The density of the normal distribution is given by:

\[f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma ^ 2}} e ^ {-\frac{1}{2}(\frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}) ^ 2}, \quad x \in \mathbf{R}.\]

The formula might look somewhat intimidating but you will find that working with it is not that hard.

We will be using the normal distribution quite often and it is inconvinient to write the density often. Therere when we refer to a variable following a normal distribution with mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\) we will simply write \(X \sim N(\mu, \sigma)\).

Let us now focus on the treatment time of a that we select at random from the population. We don’t know how many days of treatment will be required for that patient. Let us denote the unknown time with \(X\) that can take values in \(\mathbf{R}\) \((-\infty, +\infty)\). You may wonder whether negative hospital times make sense and you would be quite right, but let us defer this discussion for now. Let us further assume that \(X\) follows a normal distribution with mean \(\mu = 14\) and standard deviation \(\sigma = 6\) and write for short \(X \sim N(14, 6 ^ 2)\).

A plot of the density function of \(N(14, 6 ^ 2)\) looks like this:

The expected value of \(X\) is given by:

\[E(X) = \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} xf(x)dx\]

it can be shown that the expected value of a normally distributed variable is:

\[E(X) = \mu\]

it can futher be shown that the variance of the X is:

\[Var(X) = \sigma ^ 2\] Closely related to the variance is the standard deviation which is simply the square root of the variance:

\[Sd(X) = \sqrt{Var(X)} = \sigma\]

Both the expected value and the variance are summaries of the distribution function that measure specific aspects of that function. Think of the expected value as the center of the distribution and of the variance as its spread. Experiment with the code and plot a couple of density functions with different parameters. You will notice that changing \(\mu\) will move the curve along the \(x\)-axis while changing \(\sigma\) affects its shape: the lower the standard deviation the steeper the bell (XXX) curve.

Theorem 4.1 (Symmetry of the normal distribution) The normal distribution is symmetrical around its expected value. Let \(a\) be any real number. \[\begin{align} f(\mu + a) = f(\mu - a) \end{align}\]
Proof. \[\begin{align} f(\mu + a) & = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma ^ 2}} e ^ {\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\mu + a - \mu}{\sigma}\right) ^ 2} \\ & = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma ^ 2}} e ^ {\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{a}{\sigma}\right) ^ 2} \\ & = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma ^ 2}} e ^ {\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{-a}{\sigma}\right) ^ 2} \\ & = f(\mu - a) \end{align}\]

The area under the density function gives the probability of events.

Technically, under some regularity conditions, the expected value of a distribution (we will also speac of the expected value of a random variable following a given distribution) is given by:

\[\begin{align} E(X) = \int_{-\infty} ^ {+\infty} x f(x) dx. \end{align}\]

The normal distribution with \(\mu = 0\) and \(\sigma = 1\) is called the standard normal distribution. A normally distributed random variable \(X \sim N(\mu, \sigma ^ 2)\) can be standardized by subtracting its mean and deviding by its standard deviation.

\[\begin{align} Z & = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma} \\ E(Z) & = E\left(\frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}\right) \\ & = \frac{E(X) - \mu}{\sigma} \\ & = \frac{\mu - \mu}{\sigma} \\ & = 0 \\ Var(Z) & = Var\left(\frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}\right) \\ & = \frac{1}{\sigma ^ 2} Var(X - \mu) \\ & = \frac{\sigma ^ 2}{\sigma ^ 2} \\ & = 1 \end{align}\]

4.2 Chi square distribution

The \(\chi^2\) distributions commonly arises in statistical tests. Unlike the normal distribution, the \(\chi^2\) distribution has a single parameter called the degrees of freedom.

Definition 4.1 Let \(Z_1,\ldots,Z_{n}\) be iid standard normal variables (\(Z_i \sim N(0, 1)\)). The distribution of the sum of their squares is \(\chi^2\) with \(n\) degrees of freedom.

\[\begin{align} Z_1 ^2 + Z_2 ^ 2 + \ldots + Z_n^2 \sim \chi^2(n) \tag{4.1}. \end{align}\]

It can be shown that the expected value of a \(\chi^2(n)\) distributed variable is equal to the degrees of freedom of the distribution.

\[\begin{align} E(X) = n. \end{align}\]

It can be shown that if the variables in the sum @ref\tag{4.1} are subject to \(k\) linear restrictions of the form:

\[\begin{align*} a_{11} Z_1 + a_{12} Z_2 + \ldots + a_{1n} Z_n = b_1 \\ \vdots \\ a_{kn} Z_1 + a_{22} Z_2 + \ldots + a_{kn} Z_n = b_k \end{align*}\]

then the sum of the squared variables follows a \(\chi^2\) distribution with \(n - m\) degress of freedom.

Theorem 4.2 The sum of squared deviations from the mean of \(n\) independent normal variables \(X_1,\ldots,X_n\) with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma ^ 2\) has a \(\chi^2\) distribution with \(n - 1\) degrees of freedom.

\[\begin{align} \frac{n}{\sigma ^ 2}\sum_{i = 1} ^ {n} (X_i - \bar{X}) ^ 2 \sim \chi^2(n - 1) \tag{4.2} \end{align}\]

Note that the degress of freedom in (4.2) are \(n - 1\) and not \(n\). The proof of 4.2 is beyond the scope of this course but note that the summands in (4.2) are not independent but are required to sum to zero, because these are deviations from the sample mean.

\[\begin{align} \sum_{i = 1} ^ n (X_i - \bar{X}) & = \sum_{i = 1} ^ n X_i - \sum_{i = 1} ^ n \bar{X}\\ & = \frac{n}{n}\sum_{i = 1} ^ n X_i - n \bar{X}\\ & = n\bar{X} - n \bar{X} \\ & = 0. \end{align}\]

This is a consequence of a restriction on the summands. These are not fully independent as in the definition of the \(\chi^2\) distribution but are required to sum to zero, because these are deviations from the sample mean.

4.3 t-distribution

The t-distribution commonly arises in statistical tests and is used to model processes where extreme events are more probable that under a normal distribution. Its density function is quite involved and we will not present it here. Instead we will visualise its density and compare it to the standard normal distribution.

The \(t\)-distribution with \(n\) degress of freedom arises as the ratio between a standard normal random variable Z and a \(\chi^2\) distributed variables.

Theorem 4.3 (t distribution) Let \(Z \sim N(0, 1)\) and \(X \sim \chi ^ 2 (n)\) and let \(Z\) and \(X\) be independent.

\[\begin{align} \frac{Z}{\sqrt{X}} \sim t(n). \end{align}\]

Figure 4.3: Densities of the standard normal and t-distributions with 1 and 5 degrees of freedom.

From 4.3 you can see that the t-distribution with one degree of freedom assigns higher probability to events (intervals) far away from the center, i.e. extreme events. The probability to observe a value greater that 3 under the standard normal distribution is.

probNormGt3 <- 1 - pnorm(3, 0, 1)

\[\begin{align} P_{N(0,1)}(X > 3) = 0.0013. \end{align}\]

wherease the probability for the same event under a \(t(1)\) distribution is

probTgt3 <- 1 - pt(3, df = 1)

\[\begin{align} P_{t(1)}(X > 3) = 0.1024. \end{align}\]
